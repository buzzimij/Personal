Executive Summary — What Amazon Is in 2026

The One-Paragraph Thesis

Amazon is simultaneously the world's largest retailer, largest cloud provider, third-largest advertising platform, most sophisticated logistics operator, a leading AI infrastructure company, a healthcare provider, a satellite internet company, a content studio, and a consumer electronics maker — all in one. In FY2025, it generated $716.9 billion in revenue and $77.7 billion in net income. The central tension: the retail/logistics engine is a low-margin, capex-heavy business that generates volume and data; AWS is a high-margin ($35B+ gross profit per quarter) cash machine that funds everything else; and advertising is the surprise profit engine now generating $68B+ per year at software-like margins. The $200B capex plan for 2026 is the defining near-term story — a bet that AI infrastructure demand is structural, not cyclical, and that Amazon's custom silicon (Trainium) can reduce its NVIDIA dependency while lowering costs for customers who commit to AWS.

The Profit Concentration Reality
📊
AWS Is Amazon's Profit Engine
AWS represents 18% of Amazon's revenue but generates approximately 57% of its total operating income. The retail/logistics business ($588B in revenue) earns operating income of roughly $34B — a ~5.8% margin. AWS earns $45.6B in operating income on $128.7B revenue — a 35% margin. Amazon's total stock market valuation is largely a bet on AWS continuing to grow at 20%+ per year while the retail business generates enough cash flow to be self-sustaining. If AWS slows meaningfully, the investment thesis collapses even though retail alone is the world's largest commerce operation.

Andy Jassy & Amazon's Strategic Identity

Andy Jassy became Amazon's CEO in July 2021, succeeding Jeff Bezos. Jassy was the founding leader of AWS — he built it from scratch — which means Amazon's current CEO is, at his core, a cloud infrastructure executive. His strategic priorities since taking the helm: (1) aggressively cut costs in underperforming retail segments (2022–2023 layoffs of ~27,000 employees), (2) restore AWS growth momentum after a post-COVID slowdown, (3) position Amazon as an AI-first company rather than a cloud company that also does AI, and (4) defend Prime's value proposition against streaming competition.

💡
Jassy's Most Important Strategic Statement (2026 Shareholder Letter)
Jassy's April 2026 shareholder letter defended the $200B capex plan: "AI capacity is being monetized as quickly as it is installed. We have never seen demand like this in AWS's 20-year history. We are not over-building — we are under-building relative to what customers are asking for." This is the single most important line in Amazon's investment thesis — if true, $200B in capex generates commensurate revenue; if wrong, Amazon faces a massive capex hangover similar to what telecom companies experienced in the early 2000s.

Jassy has also been explicit that AI will reduce Amazon's total corporate headcount — Amazon has 1,000+ generative AI projects in development or deployed internally, ranging from warehouse robotics optimization to AWS customer service automation. The long-term goal: run a $1T+ revenue business with meaningfully fewer people than today through AI-driven efficiency across every function.

Why the Marketplace Economics Are the Real Story

When Amazon sells a product directly (1P), it buys inventory at wholesale, stores it, ships it, handles returns — generating roughly 1–3% gross margin. When a third-party seller sells through Amazon (3P), Amazon collects a referral fee (8–15% of sale price), FBA fulfillment fees, storage fees, and advertising spend — generating an effective take rate of approximately 30–45% of the gross merchandise value it facilitates. Amazon earns more money facilitating a $100 third-party sale than selling a $100 product directly.

💰
The Marketplace Take Rate Math
At $575B in 3P GMV and $172B in seller services revenue, Amazon captures a blended ~30% of third-party GMV as fee revenue. On a high-margin basis (these are fee streams, not product sales), this generates substantial incremental profit. The shift from 60% 3P GMV (2019) to 69% 3P GMV (2025) is one of the most structurally bullish trends in Amazon's business — every percentage point shift from 1P to 3P at current volumes generates approximately $5–6B in additional high-margin fee revenue.
The FTC Antitrust Case

The FTC's ongoing antitrust lawsuit against Amazon (filed 2023) centers on Amazon's marketplace practices: specifically, whether Amazon penalizes sellers who offer lower prices on other platforms (the "anti-discounting" allegation), and whether Amazon's "buy box" algorithm unfairly favors sellers who use FBA. The buy box suppression practice — removing "Add to Cart" buttons from sellers who raised prices due to tariff costs — became a flashpoint in early 2026 when sellers raised tariff-related prices. The FTC case is a multi-year legal process; outcomes range from behavioral remedies to structural separation. Most analysts consider the most severe outcomes (forcing Amazon to divest marketplace from retail) unlikely but not impossible.

Advertising — The $68B Business Wall Street Underestimates
Why Amazon Advertising Is Structurally Dominant

Amazon's advertising business is fundamentally different from Google's and Meta's — and likely more defensible. When a consumer searches for "running shoes" on Google, Google knows they are interested in running shoes. When a consumer searches for "running shoes" on Amazon, Amazon knows they are about to buy running shoes. This purchase-intent signal is the most commercially valuable signal in digital advertising, making Amazon advertising the highest-converting ad format in e-commerce. Brands get measurable return on ad spend directly attributable to sales, in a closed-loop system Amazon controls entirely.

Why the $244B Backlog Matters

The AWS "remaining performance obligations" backlog — meaning contracted future revenue not yet recognized — reached $244 billion at year-end 2025, up 40% year-over-year and 22% in a single quarter. This is extraordinary: it means AWS has locked in nearly two years of revenue visibility at current run rates, and the pipeline is growing faster than revenue. Enterprise customers typically sign 3–5 year cloud commitments. The $57B sequential backlog increase in Q4 2025 alone represents one of the largest single-quarter enterprise software/cloud commitments ever recorded, driven by enterprises accelerating AI infrastructure decisions before capacity constraints tighten further.

🔒
Backlog as a Leading Indicator
At $244B backlog and a $142B annualized run rate, AWS has roughly 1.7 years of revenue locked in contracts. Custom Silicon — Trainium & Inferentia: AWS's NVIDIA Alternative
Why Amazon Built Its Own Chips

Amazon's custom silicon strategy was born from two realities: (1) NVIDIA GPUs are supply-constrained and expensive; and (2) Amazon's own AI workloads — training and serving Alexa, recommendation systems, search ranking, fraud detection, and now Claude via Anthropic — are so large that even marginal efficiency gains on custom chips translate into billions of dollars in annual cost savings. The goal is not to eliminate NVIDIA from AWS — NVIDIA remains dominant and AWS sells NVIDIA instances — but to offer a credible alternative that improves AWS margins and gives customers a cost-optimized option for specific workload types.

Trainium3 features 8 NeuronCore-v4 engines per chip, each with 128×128 BF16 and 512×128 MXFP8 systolic arrays, plus 32 MiB of dedicated SRAM per engine. The liquid-cooling requirement is a deliberate design choice that reduces operating temperatures and enables higher sustained clock speeds — at the cost of requiring purpose-built data center infrastructure.

Trainium4 — The NVIDIA Hybrid Strategy (H2 2026 / Early 2027)

Trainium4 represents a significant strategic evolution. In addition to delivering approximately 6× the FP4 throughput, 3× FP8 performance, 4× more HBM4 memory bandwidth, and 2× memory capacity vs. Trainium3, Trainium4 will support NVIDIA's NVLink Fusion interconnect alongside UALink. This means Trainium4 chips can operate in the same cluster as NVIDIA GPUs — enabling "hybrid" compute clusters that combine Trainium's cost efficiency for inference with NVIDIA's Blackwell/Rubin architecture for training. This is a remarkable strategic move: rather than forcing customers to choose between Trainium and NVIDIA, Amazon is building chips that plug into NVIDIA's ecosystem. Apple, OpenAI, and Anthropic have all reportedly been piloting Amazon's Trainium chips, a significant signal of external confidence in the silicon.

Project Rainier — The World's Largest AI Cluster

Project Rainier is a dedicated Amazon compute cluster built specifically for Anthropic's Claude training. Amazon Logistics (AMZL) — From Last-Mile to Everything

Amazon Logistics began in 2015 as a last-mile delivery overflow for UPS and FedEx. Today it delivers 46% of Amazon's own packages and is expanding into third-party logistics for non-Amazon merchants. AMZL's competitive advantage is density: it delivers 6–8 packages per stop vs. UPS/FedEx at 2–3, because it only serves Amazon-concentrated urban residential routes. This density makes AMZL structurally cheaper per package than legacy carriers on those routes.

In 2025, Amazon introduced a 3.5% inbound placement surcharge for third-party sellers sending inventory to Amazon fulfillment centers — a direct revenue line that monetizes the $98.5B logistics investment. Analysts estimate this surcharge generates $3–5B in incremental annual revenue.

Rural Expansion — The Untapped Last Frontier

Amazon is investing $4 billion to triple its rural delivery network by 2026. Same-Day and Next-Day delivery — previously concentrated in top-50 metro areas — is expanding to thousands of smaller communities. This is both a competitive moat move (preventing Walmart from owning rural Prime) and an advertising move: rural Prime members who receive faster delivery renew at higher rates and spend more per order. Amazon also operates 4,000+ pickup locations (Amazon Hub, Whole Foods, Kohl's partnerships) as a low-cost alternative to last-mile residential delivery.

Why No Competitor Can Replicate This
19-Year Head Start
Amazon has been building fulfillment centers since 2005. Its current network includes 200+ fulfillment centers, 1,000+ delivery stations, and 200+ sortation centers — a $150B+ asset base built over nearly two decades. Walmart's logistics network is extensive but optimized for store replenishment, not individual package delivery.
Inventory Placement Intelligence
Amazon uses ML models to pre-position inventory closest to likely buyers before orders are placed — called "inventory regionalization." This allows same-day/next-day delivery without expensive expedited shipping. The prediction accuracy is a function of 30 years of purchase data that no new entrant can replicate.

Alexa+ — What "Agentic AI" Means in Practice

Alexa+ is a fundamentally redesigned Alexa built on a dual-model architecture: Amazon Nova handles real-time voice responsiveness and device commands (optimized for <300ms response latency), while Anthropic Claude handles complex multi-step reasoning tasks that require deep analysis, external API orchestration, and contextual memory. This split-model design is intentional — Nova is faster and cheaper per query; Claude is more capable for complex tasks. Together they enable what Amazon calls "agentic AI" — Alexa+ can autonomously complete multi-step goals across services without the user needing to issue each micro-command.

Agentic Capability Examples
  • Book a restaurant via OpenTable — searches menus, checks availability, confirms booking
  • Order an Uber Eats — navigates app, selects restaurant, places order, tracks delivery
  • Text a babysitter — drafts, reviews context, sends via Messages integration
  • Reschedule a doctor appointment — navigates One Medical calendar, finds new slot
  • Set up smart home routines — chains 20+ device actions from a single natural language request
  • Compare insurance quotes — fills forms on third-party sites, returns ranked options

Devices as a Commerce and Data Flywheel

Amazon's devices business has never been primarily a hardware-margin business — it operates devices at or near cost, monetizing through subscription revenue (Prime, Kindle Unlimited, Music Unlimited), advertising (Fire TV ads, Alexa skills ads), and purchase data. A household that owns an Echo, Fire TV, Ring, and Kindle is deeply embedded in the Amazon ecosystem. Alexa+ expands this further: every interaction — voice search, appointment booking, grocery order, home automation command — generates behavioral data that refines both advertising targeting and product recommendations.
