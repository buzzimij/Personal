Section 01

Executive Summary

Core Finding

Connecticut House Bill 5043, the Convertible Pistol Act, is projected to pass the Connecticut Senate on May 6, 2026 — the final day of the legislative session — and receive Governor Ned Lamont's signature within days. The bill bans the commercial sale, import, manufacture, and advertisement of any semiautomatic pistol whose internal trigger mechanism (the "cruciform trigger bar") can be exploited by an illegal auto-conversion device, commonly called a "Glock switch." The ban does not apply to existing owners, who may retain their firearms indefinitely.

The legislation targets a genuine public safety crisis — Glock switch seizures in Connecticut rose dramatically between 2023 and 2025, and the devices have been linked to 20 U.S. mass shootings — but its design-based definition is broad enough to create substantial regulatory uncertainty for the entire striker-fired pistol market, not only Glock-branded firearms. The Ruger RXM was formally threatened by Connecticut's Attorney General six months before the bill's introduction; dozens of other Glock-compatible aftermarket platforms share the same internal architecture and fall within the bill's plain text.

Dealers face a hard October 1, 2026 deadline to clear inventory. Glock sales in Connecticut have already surged 33% year-over-year as consumers and dealers anticipate the ban. NRA-ILA litigation is expected to follow California's analogous challenge in Jaynes v. Bonta.

Section 02

Background and Context

2.1 The Glock Switch Problem

The "Glock switch" — officially termed a "pistol converter" or "auto sear" in the bill's language — is a small metal or plastic device, roughly the size of a quarter, that attaches to the rear of a semiautomatic pistol's slide. When installed, it physically depresses the pistol's cruciform trigger bar from outside the frame, bypassing the firearm's semi-automatic reset mechanism and converting the weapon to fire continuously with a single trigger pull. A standard Glock 17 loaded with a 17-round magazine can be emptied in under one second using a switch device, at a theoretical rate of up to 1,200 rounds per minute.

The vulnerability was first identified by Venezuelan gunsmith Jorge Leon in the 1980s, when he noticed that Glock's simplified firing assembly — designed to reduce part count and improve reliability — created a path for external interference with the trigger bar. For decades, the complexity of machining the necessary component limited access. By 2020, advances in CNC machining and 3D printing, combined with Chinese manufacturing, reduced the market price of a Glock switch to between $20 and $50, with units readily available online. U.S. Customs and Border Protection seized over 1,500 switch devices at ports of entry in 2023 alone.

Why this is not a new crime: Glock switches are already illegal under federal law. Under the National Firearms Act of 1986, any device that converts a semiautomatic firearm to fire automatically is classified as a machine gun, and civilian acquisition of machine guns manufactured after May 1986 is prohibited. Possession of a Glock switch is a federal felony. The Connecticut bill does not address the switch device itself — it targets the underlying pistol design that makes the switch effective.

2.2 Connecticut-Specific Enforcement Context

Hartford, Bridgeport, and New Haven police departments reported a combined 51 Glock switch seizures between 2023 and 2024, with the devices appearing in drive-by shootings, carjackings, and gang-related homicides. Hartford Police Chief Vernon Reddick became a prominent public advocate for the legislation, testifying at the Judiciary Committee that modified Glocks "turn every street crime into a potential mass casualty event" due to the difficulty of predicting when a shooter will discharge the weapon's entire magazine in a single trigger pull.

Connecticut Attorney General William Tong separately pursued firearms manufacturers under the state's Firearms Liability Act (FIRA) — Connecticut's 2023 law holding manufacturers liable for harms from weapons marketed in a negligent manner. In November 2025, Tong formally threatened Ruger with litigation over the RXM pistol, specifically because Ruger marketed the firearm as having "most major components designed for Glock Gen 3 compatibility" — a statement AG Tong characterized as affirmatively marketing the weapon's convertibility potential. The AG's action predated HB 5043 by three months and created the political momentum for the governor's bill.

2.3 National Legislative Context

Connecticut is not acting in isolation. California Governor Gavin Newsom signed an analogous convertible pistol ban in September 2025, taking effect July 1, 2026. Maryland enacted comparable legislation in April 2026. Connecticut's bill, if signed, would take effect October 1, 2026 — making it the third U.S. state with this type of legislation. The NRA-ILA filed a federal lawsuit challenging the California law (Jaynes v. Bonta, C.D. Cal.) in October 2025. The case is pending; no preliminary injunction has been granted as of this report's date. The Connecticut law would face a similar challenge and similar legal analysis.

Section 03

Legislative Analysis

3.1 Bill Text and Definitions

Bill Number: Connecticut HB 5043 (Governor's Bill No. 5043, 2026 Regular Session)
Full Title: An Act Concerning Convertible Pistols, Unfinished Frames or Unfinished Lower Receivers, Voluntary Relinquishment of Firearms and Ammunition, and Refundability of Permits for Sale at Retail of Firearms and for Carrying of Pistols and Revolvers
Introduced: February 5, 2026
Author: Governor Ned Lamont (via Joint Rule 9 executive bill mechanism)

The bill's operative prohibition rests entirely on two interconnected definitions. Every determination of whether a specific pistol model is captured by the ban flows through these definitions:

"Convertible pistol" means: any semiautomatic pistol with a cruciform trigger bar that can be readily converted by hand or with a common household tool into a machine gun solely by the installation or attachment of a pistol converter.
"Cruciform trigger bar" means: a gun component that serves as a linkage between the trigger and firing pin, with its sear incorporated in a cross-shaped surface.
"Pistol converter" means: any device or instrument that, when installed in or attached to the rear of the slide of a semiautomatic pistol, interferes with the trigger mechanism, and in so doing enables the pistol to discharge a number of shots or bullets rapidly or automatically with one continuous pull of the trigger.

Statutory Exemptions

A pistol is not a "convertible pistol" if either of the following conditions is met:

  1. Hammer-fired mechanism: The pistol uses a hammer to strike the firing pin rather than a striker. Hammer-fired pistols do not use a cruciform trigger bar and cannot physically accept Glock-type switch devices.
  2. Shielded trigger bar: The pistol's cruciform trigger bar is protected by "a tab or other piece of material molded to the pistol's frame that shields that bar from interference by a pistol converter." This is the design-modification pathway for manufacturers who wish to bring existing models into compliance.

Prohibited Conduct

Effective October 1, 2026, it is a Class D felony (maximum 5 years imprisonment, $5,000 fine, or both) for any person or business to:

  • Import a convertible pistol into Connecticut
  • Advertise, sell, offer for sale, or expose for sale a convertible pistol
  • Manufacture a convertible pistol for sale

What the Bill Does NOT Prohibit

  • Continued ownership of convertible pistols lawfully acquired before October 1, 2026
  • Private resale between individuals (not through a dealer)
  • Use, transport, or display of a lawfully owned convertible pistol
  • Out-of-state purchase (residents may legally purchase in other states where the weapon is sold, subject to applicable transfer rules)
Notable gap: No explicit law enforcement exemption. Multiple opposition witnesses, including firearms dealers and attorneys, noted that HB 5043 as passed by the House contained no carved-out exception for law enforcement agencies purchasing service weapons. Police departments in Connecticut that issue Glock pistols to officers could not purchase new units in-state after October 1, 2026. It is unclear whether the Senate's adopted "House Amendment Schedule A" adds an LE carve-out. Police chiefs who publicly supported the bill did not publicly address this gap.

3.2 Secondary Provisions

Ghost Gun Loophole Closure: The bill amends Connecticut's 2019 ghost gun statute to explicitly classify unfinished frames and 80% lower receivers as "firearms" under state law, requiring them to undergo a federal background check at point of sale. This provision closes a parallel loophole that allowed buyers to legally acquire "80% complete" Polymer80-type frames online without background checks, then complete them at home into functional firearms with no serial number.

Voluntary Relinquishment: The bill creates a statutory framework for firearms owners who wish to voluntarily surrender convertible pistols to law enforcement, including procedures for reimbursement using state-provided vouchers. This provision was not widely reported but represents a buy-back infrastructure that could be activated by subsequent legislation.

Permit Refundability: The bill allows partial refunds of firearms retail dealer permits and carry permit fees in certain circumstances where the permit holder ceases operations or surrenders their license — a provision relevant to dealers who may exit the pistol market following the ban's implementation.

Affected Firearms: Complete Platform Analysis

5.1 Methodology

The bill does not list specific manufacturers or model numbers. It applies a functional, design-based test: does the pistol contain a cruciform trigger bar, and can it be converted to fire automatically using a pistol converter without specialized tools? Every model determination below is based on one or more of the following sources: (1) explicit naming in legislative floor debate or Judiciary Committee testimony; (2) Connecticut Attorney General formal action or threat letter; (3) manufacturer marketing disclosures confirming Glock-compatible internal components; or (4) publicly documented technical analysis by firearms engineers identifying cruciform trigger bar presence.

The regulatory authority to make final platform-specific determinations rests with the Connecticut Commissioner of Emergency Services and Public Protection (or the courts in litigation). The analysis below represents the current state of publicly available information and should not substitute for legal advice.

Section 06

Constitutional and Legal Exposure

6.1 Second Amendment Framework — Post-Bruen Analysis

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen fundamentally restructured Second Amendment analysis. Under Bruen, a firearms regulation is constitutional only if: (1) the regulated conduct falls outside the Second Amendment's scope, or (2) the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States at the time of the Fourteenth Amendment's ratification (1868). The pre-Bruen interest-balancing tests — weighing government interests against individual rights — are no longer valid.

Connecticut's defense against a Second Amendment challenge will likely rest on two theories. Theory 1: Machine gun carve-out. The state will argue that a convertible pistol is effectively a machine gun — a weapon category that has never enjoyed Second Amendment protection and that the Supreme Court in Heller explicitly noted as outside the Second Amendment's core. If courts accept that the pistol's convertibility makes it categorically equivalent to a machine gun, the ban survives without historical analysis. Theory 2: Dangerous/unusual weapon. Under Heller, weapons that are "dangerous and unusual" are outside Second Amendment protection. The state will argue that a pistol capable of firing 1,200 rounds per minute qualifies.

The NRA's challenge in Jaynes v. Bonta (California) directly contests these theories, arguing that the regulation targets the untransformed pistol — an ordinary semiautomatic firearm — based on a potential modification that is already separately illegal. The pistol itself, before any switch is attached, is an unremarkable handgun covered by Heller's protection of the "quintessential self-defense weapon." As of this report's date, no preliminary injunction has been issued in Jaynes, suggesting courts have not found a clear likelihood of success on the merits for the challengers.

Arguments Supporting Constitutionality

  • Convertible pistols are effectively machine guns; Heller explicitly excludes machine guns from Second Amendment protection
  • The ability to convert to automatic fire makes these weapons "dangerous and unusual" outside Heller's core protection
  • The ban affects only commercial sale, not possession — a meaningful limitation on scope
  • California's analogous law survived preliminary injunction review (as of this report's date)
  • Historical tradition of regulating automatic-fire weapons dates to the NFA in 1934

Arguments Against Constitutionality

  • The regulated object is an untransformed semiautomatic pistol — the quintessential Heller-protected weapon — not a machine gun
  • The Bruen historical tradition test is difficult to satisfy for design-based restrictions without a clear 1868 analog
  • The broad design definition captures pistols (M&P, XD) with no documented history of switch conversion — overbroad regulation
  • Regulatory uncertainty about which models are captured may violate due process vagueness doctrine
  • No law enforcement exemption creates equal protection concerns

6.2 Dealer and Industry Legal Exposure

Connecticut firearms dealers face two distinct legal risks beginning October 1, 2026. The first is criminal: selling a covered pistol after the effective date is a Class D felony. The second is civil: Connecticut's Firearms Liability Act (FIRA) imposes civil liability on manufacturers and sellers who negligently market firearms. CT AG Tong has already demonstrated willingness to use FIRA aggressively (Ruger RXM threat letter, November 2025). A dealer who sells a borderline-Tier-2 pistol after the effective date and receives a regulatory determination that it was covered could face simultaneous criminal and civil exposure.

Dealers should seek written legal opinions on any Tier-2 platform before October 1, 2026, and should monitor the state's anticipated regulatory guidance on covered models. The bill does not establish a formal administrative process for pre-clearance of specific models, which is itself a significant compliance gap.

Section 07

Market Impact Analysis

7.1 Anticipatory Buying Surge

Connecticut handgun sales were up 33% year-over-year in March 2026, the fourth-largest jump nationally, according to NICS (National Instant Criminal Background Check System) data. This follows a well-documented pattern in anticipatory firearms purchasing ahead of announced legislative bans. Glock sales specifically surged to approximately 30% of total pistol sales in Connecticut gun shops in the weeks following the April 22 House passage. Gun dealers across the state reported customers explicitly citing the October 1, 2026 deadline as their motivation for immediate purchase.

The practical implication: significant inventory liquidation is already underway. Dealers with heavy Glock stock prior to the announcement are in a favorable position; dealers who relied on just-in-time ordering may struggle to source sufficient inventory before October 1 as distributors prioritize markets without the ban.

7.2 Long-Term Connecticut Market Restructuring

Glock holds approximately 35–40% of U.S. semiautomatic pistol market share nationally. In Connecticut, the elimination of new Glock sales from dealer shelves represents a structural market shift of significant magnitude. The gap will be filled by hammer-fired platforms (Beretta 92-series, SIG P226/P229, CZ 75-series, 1911-pattern), striker-fired platforms with different architecture (SIG P320/P365, HK VP9, Walther PDP — subject to regulatory confirmation of exemption), and revolvers. Dealers in Connecticut will need to substantially restructure their handgun inventory mix within five months.
